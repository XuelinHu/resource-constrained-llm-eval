\subsection{Baseline Results}

\Cref{tab:main-results} summarizes the completed public benchmark export. The
strongest completed baselines are Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct and Qwen3-8B. In the
current best-available precision rows, Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct reaches 0.718 on
MMLU, 0.796 on C-Eval, and 0.665 on HumanEval. Qwen3-8B reaches 0.718 on MMLU,
0.781 on C-Eval, and 0.659 on HumanEval. Qwen3-4B is the strongest smaller
mid-scale baseline in the completed table, with 0.681 on MMLU, 0.715 on
C-Eval, and 0.652 on HumanEval.

The smaller Qwen3 models show the expected quality-efficiency pattern.
Qwen3-1.7B improves substantially over Qwen3-0.6B, especially on MMLU and
HumanEval, while still remaining much cheaper than 7B/8B models. GSM8K is zero
or near-zero across the current export. This should be treated as an evaluation
configuration issue or strict-answer-extraction warning until the math task is
rechecked with the final prompting policy.

\subsection{Efficiency Results}

\Cref{tab:efficiency-results} shows that low-bit inference does not
automatically improve runtime on the tested stack. For the Qwen3 family, int4
substantially reduces peak allocated VRAM: Qwen3-8B drops from 15.30GB in bf16
to 5.78GB in int4, and Qwen3-4B drops from 7.54GB to 2.55GB. However, bf16 is
faster in the current efficiency prompt set. Qwen3-8B produces 39.69 tokens/s
in bf16 and 22.46 tokens/s in int4; Qwen3-4B produces 38.74 tokens/s in bf16
and 22.38 tokens/s in int4.

The smallest models are most attractive when memory and speed dominate.
Qwen3-0.6B uses 1.15GB in bf16 and reaches 54.28 tokens/s, while Qwen3-1.7B
uses 3.24GB and reaches 52.88 tokens/s. The 7B-class Qwen2.5 and DeepSeek rows
fit within 24GB, but their int4 memory reductions are modest in the current
export. This reinforces the need to measure the actual backend rather than
assuming a fixed theoretical compression ratio.

\subsection{QLoRA Adaptation Results}

\Cref{tab:qlora-results} reports the current adapter evaluation export. The
adapter run confirms that QLoRA training and evaluation can be executed in the
single-GPU workflow, but the exported Domain QA score is still based on one
test sample and is therefore not a final domain-effectiveness result. For
Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, the adapter export preserves public benchmark quality
reasonably well relative to the bf16 baseline: adapted int4 MMLU is 0.710
compared with 0.718 in the bf16 baseline, and adapted int4 C-Eval is 0.789
compared with 0.796. The adapter HumanEval score is also close to the baseline
value in the raw CSV. These observations are promising for pipeline viability,
but they must be repeated with full-domain evaluation before claiming railway
adaptation gains.

\subsection{Model Selection Analysis}

The current evidence supports three deployment recommendations. First, for
accuracy-oriented single-GPU experiments, Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct and Qwen3-8B are
the strongest completed choices. Second, for speed- and memory-oriented local
services, Qwen3-0.6B and Qwen3-1.7B are the most practical lightweight
candidates. Third, Qwen3-4B is a useful middle point because it is much cheaper
than 7B/8B models while retaining competitive C-Eval and HumanEval scores.

The model recommendation may change after the full railway-domain test split is
exported. In particular, a smaller model that receives a strong QLoRA domain
gain may be preferable to a larger baseline if it answers railway terminology
queries with lower latency and memory cost. The next experimental priority is
therefore not another public benchmark run, but a full 2,770-sample Domain QA
baseline and adapter evaluation with stable answer normalization.
