Large language models are increasingly used for translation, question
answering, code assistance, and technical-document support. Railway engineering
is a natural application area because its documents contain specialized
bilingual terminology, operational regulations, inspection procedures, and
maintenance knowledge. However, practical adoption in teaching laboratories and
small research groups is constrained by hardware. A team may have access to one
consumer workstation GPU, but not to multi-GPU inference, proprietary remote
services, or large-scale full-parameter fine-tuning.

This paper studies that practical boundary directly. The target environment is
a single NVIDIA RTX 3090 GPU with 24GB of VRAM. Under this constraint, model
selection cannot be reduced to a public leaderboard score. A locally useful
model must fit into memory, answer at acceptable latency, support low-bit
inference, and remain adaptable to railway-domain data. Recent open models such
as Qwen~\cite{qwen3,qwen25}, DeepSeek~\cite{deepseekllm,deepseekr1},
Mistral~\cite{mistral7b}, Phi~\cite{phi3}, Yi~\cite{yi2024open},
Gemma~\cite{gemma2024open,gemma2,gemma3}, and GLM-style Chinese-oriented
models make this local deployment setting realistic, but they still require
consistent measurement under the actual software and hardware stack.

The study is positioned as an AI application and experimental evaluation paper,
not as a new model-architecture paper. We evaluate locally cached open LLMs on
public benchmarks and railway-domain tasks, measure efficiency under low-bit
settings, and test QLoRA adaptation for selected models. The current
manuscript is also explicit about evidence maturity. Some experiments have
already been exported as CSV and JSON artifacts; others remain planned in the
latest work plan. Rather than hiding this boundary, the draft marks unfinished
experiments as placeholders so that the paper can be completed systematically.

The work is guided by five research questions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Which local open LLMs can be deployed stably under a single RTX 3090
  24GB constraint?
  \item How do low-bit inference settings affect public benchmark quality,
  railway-domain quality, memory footprint, latency, and throughput?
  \item Do public benchmark scores correlate with railway-domain QA and
  regulatory QA behavior?
  \item Can QLoRA improve domain behavior while preserving useful public
  benchmark performance under the same 24GB constraint?
  \item Which data composition is preferable for adaptation: Domain-QA,
  Domain-RegQA, a mixed dataset, or a completion-only mixed variant?
\end{enumerate}

The contributions of this draft are:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item We define a ten-model local LLM pool for a single RTX 3090 study and
  align the manuscript tables with that latest plan.
  \item We document two railway-domain datasets: Domain-QA for bilingual
  terminology and translation, and Domain-RegQA for evidence-grounded
  regulatory question answering.
  \item We report the measurements already exported in the project, including
  int4 public and domain results for Qwen3-4B and Qwen3-8B, efficiency rows for
  selected models, and QLoRA Adapter-A artifacts.
  \item We reorganize the paper into a complete empirical structure with
  explicit placeholders for the remaining ten-model sweep, mixed-adapter
  ablation, and final full-domain comparisons.
  \item We maintain a normalized BibTeX library and literature-method matrix
  covering open model families, resource-efficient deployment,
  parameter-efficient adaptation, pruning, federated learning, and
  resource-constrained applications.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of the paper follows a PeerJ Computer Science application-paper
structure. \Cref{sec:related_work} reviews model evaluation, efficient
adaptation, and constrained deployment. \Cref{sec:problem_setting} defines the
railway QA scenario. \Cref{sec:datasets} summarizes the two local datasets.
\Cref{sec:methodology} describes the evaluation and adaptation workflow.
\Cref{sec:experimental_setup} presents the hardware, model pool, and metrics.
\Cref{sec:results} reports completed results and placeholders, and
\Cref{sec:discussion} discusses deployment implications and limitations.
